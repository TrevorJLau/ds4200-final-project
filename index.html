Visualizing Environmental Inequities:
Pollution and Household Income Dynamics

Overall Introduction

Exploring the intricate relationship between environmental pollution and median household income in the United States is vital for understanding the broader dynamics of environmental justice and economic inequality. Pollution, manifesting in various forms such as air and water contaminants, does not impact all communities equally. It disproportionately affects those in lower economic brackets, highlighting a stark intersection of environmental degradation and social inequality. This complex interplay reveals how geographical location and economic status can influence exposure to environmental risks, making it a crucial area of study for advocating for equitable environmental policies and practices.

Through the lens of interactive visualizations, this project aims to illuminate the patterns, trends, and correlations between pollution levels across different pollutants and median household income. By visualizing these dynamics, we can uncover the nuanced ways in which environmental harm and economic conditions intersect, providing a clearer understanding of the distribution of environmental burdens. Such insights are essential for formulating targeted interventions aimed at mitigating pollution exposure in vulnerable communities and fostering a more sustainable and equitable future for all.

Addressing environmental inequities requires a comprehensive examination of the factors at play, including economic disparities, regulatory frameworks, and the historical context of land use and industrial placement. By correlating pollution data with household income, this project seeks to highlight areas of concern where environmental injustices may be most pronounced. This endeavor not only sheds light on the current state of environmental equity but also serves as a call to action for policymakers, community leaders, and citizens to engage in concerted efforts towards addressing these critical issues, ensuring that environmental health and prosperity are accessible to all, regardless of economic standing.

To further explore the relationship between environmental pollution and socioeconomic factors, consider visiting the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) for comprehensive pollution data and insights. For in-depth analyses on income disparities and socioeconomic research, the U.S. Census Bureau offers a wealth of data, including detailed household income statistics. Additionally, policy research and environmental justice initiatives can be found at the Environmental Justice Network and Union of Concerned Scientists, where you can access a range of studies and reports aimed at addressing environmental inequities and advocating for sustainable policies.

The Relationship Between Pollution Rate and Median Income

Moreover, we also want to examine the relationship between pollution rate and median household income In order to do so, we ran a regression on each pollutant with state median income for 2018 data.

Based on the regressions shown by the plots, we don't see a clear pattern, suggesting that there may not be a straightforward or strong linear relationship between median income and pollution levels for any of the pollutants. In fact, we noticed that there is even a downward slope for O3, suggesting that higher income levels may even be associated with factors that contribute to lower O3 emissions, such as newer vehicles with better emission controls, transitions to cleaner energy sources, or more effective environmental policies.

We have also created a line plot to show how the pollutant levels changed over time. For these charts, we show the full range of pollutant data from 2000 to 2021. Firstly, we are able to see a steady decrease in mean pollutant levels for carbon monoxide, nitrogen dioxide, and sulfur dioxide, with each at least 50 percent lower than their highs in the early 2000s. However, we cannot see the same decrease in ground ozone levels which have remained relatively consistent the last 21 years. This may be due to ground ozone not usually being emitted directly, but rather forms from chemical reactions between nitrogen oxides and volatile organic compounds in sunlight. Since it is usually not man-made, the advent of stricter emission regulations in the 2000s might not have had the same limiting effect on ground ozone compared to other air pollutants.
